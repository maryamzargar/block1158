BLOCK 1158

Upper West Side, among other neighborhoods in New York City, has been subjected to many changes over that past century. From a lunatic asylum on one corner to an intellectual and cultural hub on another Upper West Side has undergone some drastic shifts that essentially have shaped the neighborhood’s character. We learn that even today, the neighborhood is subjected to further change, despite the numerous effective neighborhood coalitions that the residents of the community have formed. While change in the context on New York City is inevitable, who in the community it affects and how it unfolds make it essential to study. The study looks at the overall changes in the Upper West Side while mostly focusing on the southern side of the neighborhood where the Lincoln Square is located. Furthermore, the study is conducted while considering historical, socio-political and economic factors that influenced the physical structure of the neighborhood from 1940s to the current day.

1950

The San Juan Hill

In the late 1950’s the area from 60th to 65th Streets between Amsterdam Avenue and the 11th Avenue railroad tracks, was San Juan Hill. It was largely an Afro-American and Puerto Rican neighborhood. San Juan Hill was one of the largest and poorest African-American neighborhoods in Manhattan before the rise of Harlem. It was at this time that the African-American population exodus to Harlem and a wave of Puerto Rican residents moved in. The gang activities and the poor conditions of the neighborhood inspired Leonard Bernstein and Stephen Sondheim write the West Side Story in the late 1950’s. In 1955 John D. Rockefeller 3rd, one of the principal contributors, began with funds for planning of Lincoln Center.

1960

Urban Renwal

In 1960 the construction of Lincoln Center as a large ‘urban renewal’ project led by Robert Moses resulted in some of the African Americans living in the area to be moved to the Amsterdam Housing Projects on the far West side; the rest were displaced completely. 1970

Single Occupancy Residences

In 1970 Upper West Side saw an influx of mentally ill throughout the community and the market for Single Occupied Residents (SRO) arose. Later in the decade the Lincoln Amsterdam Housing Apartments were also constructed. After the J-51 tax abatement became available in New York, SRO units began to disappear quickly, particularly in gentrifying neighborhoods like the Upper West Side. The displacement of permanent tenants eventually became so severe that in 1973 the Mayor, John Lindsay, established a unit, Mayor’s Office of SRO Housing, to only manage the needs of SRO tenants. The unit’s responsibilities were to monitor and inspect SROs while advising the Mayor on policy issues related to SRO housing and advocating on behalf of SRO tenants with sub-standard living conditions who faced eviction and harassment.

1970 - Now

Upper West Side

In 1980’s a short-lived freight railroad, called the Miller Railroad, was nearby over 12th Avenue. It later became the elevated West Side Highway which was demolished in the same decade. These elevated structures reportedly helped make the extreme West Side a haven for violence. It was in 1990 that Upper West Side received its first historic designation limiting construction in those zones. Since then, neighborhood organizations such as Landmark West! continued to add many more buildings and areas to the list of designated landmarks and historic zones. By doing so, they have work to protect these buildings from insensitive change and demolition. In 2006, as a result of transfer of development rights, the owners of the synagogue located on the Lincoln Square agreed to swap location on the lot to make room for a new apartment tower to be built by Continental Properties. The developer in turn delivered a new synagogue with better facilities on the new location 250 feet away from the old synagogue. The Lincoln Square Synagogue is array of other buildings that has been approached by a developer to apply TDR for further development. A recent proposal concerning Air Rights of another historic building is the example of Shearith Israel farther up in Upper West Side. In this case the community groups fought to preserve the contextual zoning of the area. The project has been put on hold after numerous attempts by the developer through the Board of Standards and Appeals to propose a proper proposal that meets the community’s demands. Mayor De Blasio in 2015 proposed two zoning amendment requiring Mandatory Inclusionary Housing for new development and a Zoning for Quality and Affordability to essentially lead to FAR increase in certain zones and consequently allow for more senior and affordable housing. Although both proposals were opposed by the majority of the community boards in NYC and caused a citywide controversy during they ULURP review, they were both passed with some modification in March 2016 by City Council. While the zoning amendments do not impact Upper West Side as much as other neighborhoods they would have some ramifications nonetheless.
